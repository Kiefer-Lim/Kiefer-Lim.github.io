Words from the Creole English of Singapore and Southern Malaysia

I have a knack for sprinkling words in my everyday speech that originated from Malay, Hokkien and Cantonese in the same way that many Chinese people in the Southern Malaysian Peninsular speak colloquially. Here is a list of words I use as well as their meaning, as I myself struggle to recall the proper English terms (which is why I would use them in the first place!).

  1. Aiya: See hai-ah
  2. Ajinomoto : Monosodium Glutamate (From the Japanese company that manufactures seasoning, of the same name.)
  3. 無變 bô-piàn : There is nothing to be done; one can't do anything about it. Singaporean Hokkien term, probably influenced by Teochew.
  4. 凊彩 chhìn-chhái: (adverb) casually, as one pleases, at random ; (adjective) casual, easygoing ; any, whichever ; careless, irresponsible. Hokkien term borrowed by Cantonese and Malay.
  5. 落氣 làu-khùi : To lose face, to bring shame to one's self. Hokkien term, same meaning as 瀉衰 sià-soe.
  6. 歹勢 pháiⁿ-sè : (Interjection) Pardon me, excuse me, sorry; (Adjective) Embarrassed, shy, ill at ease. Hokkien term. Malay equivalent would be malu.
  7. Sakai : Unsophisticated person, uncultured, fool. Malay offensive, ethnic slur that 'refers to the indigenous Senoi people of Malaysia and other Orang Asli tribes'.
  8. 濕濕碎 sap1 sap1 seoi3 : Trivial; piece of cake; no big deal (semantic tone is stronger than 濕碎). Cantonese term.
  9. 瀉衰 sià-soe : To embarass, to disgrace. Hakka, Teochew, Malaysian/Singaporean Hokkien term. The prestigious Xiamen dialect of Hokkien would use 落氣 làu-khùi.
  10. Where got [ˈwɛː˦ ɡɔt˨]: Where in the world, where on Earth? See the Wiktionary explanation for ‘Where got’.
