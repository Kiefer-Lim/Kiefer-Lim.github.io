Words from the Creole English of Singapore and Southern Malaysia

I have a knack for sprinkling words in my everyday speech that originated from Malay, Hokkien and Cantonese in the same way that many Chinese people in the Southern Malaysian Peninsular speak colloquially. Here is a list of words I use as well as their meaning, as I myself struggle to recall the proper English terms (which is why I would use them in the first place!).

Many of the definitions are lifted directly from the Wiktionary entries and are not my own words.

  1. Aiya: See 害矣 (hāi--ah)
  2. Ajinomoto : Monosodium Glutamate (From the Japanese company that manufactures seasoning, of the same name.)
  3. Alamak: Oh my god, Mother of God. Malay term, possibly a contraction of Allah (god) and mak (mother), meaning Mother of God, possibly of Portugese influence.
  4. 無變 bô-piàn : There is nothing to be done; one can't do anything about it. Singaporean Hokkien term, probably influenced by Teochew.
  5. Celaka: (Adjective, Interjection) wretched, unlucky. Malay term.
  6. 插潲 chhap-siâu: (Verb) to bother, to pay attention, to meddle with. Hokkien vulgar word. (Use 插 chhap to mean pay attention; or to meddle with, without vulgar intonation.)
  7. 凊彩 chhìn-chhái: (adverb) casually, as one pleases, at random ; (adjective) casual, easygoing ; any, whichever ; careless, irresponsible. Hokkien term borrowed by Cantonese and Malay.
  8. Fuiyoh: Popularized by the Malaysian comedian, Nigel Ng, using his comedic alter ego, Uncle Roger. See 媠唷 (súi--ió).
  9. 緊張 gan2 zoeng1, kán-chiong : Nervous, anxious, keyed up. Cantonese term, whose pronunciation is borrowed by Singaporean Hokkien.
  10. 搞掂 gaau2 dim6 (Mandarin: 搞定 gǎodìng) : To get something done, to finish, to complete (a task); to settle everything, to fix a situation; to take care of a situation. Cantonese term. Mandarin borrowed this term as 搞定 gǎodìng, rendering the -m final not found in Mandarin as an -ng.
  11. 我苦 góa-khó͘ : (Interjection) Oh my God; my God; my goodness. Hokkien, Teochew term.
  12. 我老的 góa lāu--ê; (Mandarin) 哇老誒 wà láo ei, (English) Walau eh, (Malay) walao : What the hell. Literally means 'Oh my elders', Hokkien term, common among Singapore, Malaysian speakers. Mandarin writers may render this as 哇老誒 wà láo ei, while English writers often write 'walau eh'. Don't confuse with Malay's 'walau', meaning 'regardless, although', from Arabic walaw, meaning 'albeit'.
  13. 害矣 hāi--ah : An exclamation of exasperation, said especially when a bad event occurs. Chiefly Malaysian, from Hokkien.
  14. 鹹濕 haam4 sap1 : (Adjective) lecherous, lascivious; pornographic, erotic; (Verb) to sexually harass, to engage in sex acts. Cantonese term, often used as a slur.
  15. 家婆 ke-pô (English: kaypoh): (Adjective, verb) busybody; to pry into other's business. Hokkien, slang in Singlish and Manglish.
  16. 驚死 kiaⁿ-sí (English: kiasi): (Adjective) Unwilling to take a risk for fear that something bad or unfavorable will happen; cowardly, overly cautious and risk-averse; (Noun) a kiasi person. Singlish colloquial term, mildly derogatory, from Hokkien.
  17. 驚輸 kiaⁿ-su (English: kiasu): (Adjective) Afraid to lose out, particularly because one is overly competitive; (Noun) a kiasu person. Singlish colloquial term, mildly derogatory, from Hokkien.
  18. 落氣 làu-khùi : To lose face, to bring shame to one's self. Hokkien term, same meaning as 瀉衰 sià-soe.
  19. 迫促 pek-chhek : (Adjective) Annoyed; exasperated; frustrated; stressed; uneasy; terribly upset; fed up. Teochew, Singaporean Hokkien term.
  20. 歹勢 pháiⁿ-sè : (Interjection) Pardon me, excuse me, sorry; (Adjective) Embarrassed, shy, ill at ease. Hokkien term. Malay equivalent would be malu.
  21. 跋繳 poa̍h-kiáu : (Verb) to gamble. Hokkien, Teochew term.
  22. 仆街 puk1 gaai1 : (Verb) To be broke, to have no money. Malaysian, Singaporean Cantonese slang; Has other vulgar meanings in Hong Kong Cantonese.
  23. Sakai : Unsophisticated person, uncultured, fool. Malay slang that is an offensive, ethnic slur which 'refers to the indigenous Senoi people of Malaysia and other Orang Asli tribes'.
  24. 濕濕碎 sap1 sap1 seoi3 : Trivial; piece of cake; no big deal (semantic tone is stronger than 濕碎). Cantonese term.
  25. 死爸 sí-pē : (Adverb) very; rather; quite, "to the extent that one's father dies". Singapore, Malaysian Teochew, Hokkien; mildly vulgar in Manglish, Singlish.
  26. 瀉衰 sià-soe : To embarass, to disgrace. Hakka, Teochew, Malaysian/Singaporean Hokkien term. The prestigious Xiamen dialect of Hokkien would use 落氣 làu-khùi.
  27. 媠唷 súi--ió : ‘Beautiful!’， an exclamation to express confirmation or admiration. Possibly the etymology of Nigel Ng's 'Fuiyoh'.
  28. Swee: Great, neat, nice, excellent; (rarer) Neat and tidy; (rarer) Pretty, good-looking. Singlish, from Hokkien 媠 (súi, “pretty”).
  29. 豬哥伯 tir-ko-peh : licentious and lecherous man. Hokkien slur.
  30. Where got [ˈwɛː˦ ɡɔt˨]: Where in the world, where on Earth? See the Wiktionary explanation for ‘Where got’.
